Introduction

Introduction

A story is true. A story is untrue. As time extends, it matters less and less. The stories we want to believe, those are the ones that survive, despite upheaval and transition and progress. Those are the stories that shape history.

Jack Rackham in Black Sails, XXXVIII.

 

In 1883, Robert Louis Stevenson published a boys’ adventure novel that would not only become one of the classics of the genre (Gubar 2009: 69) but also enshrine enduring pirate tropes and iconography such as the peg leg and the parrot on the captain’s shoulder into the common imaginary (Cordingly 1995: 7). Treasure Island follows boy-hero Jim Hawkins in his quest for the treasure of the infamous Captain Flint, buried on a deserted island. Jim has a treasure map obtained from Billy Bones, one of Flint’s former crewmates; he is accompanied and eventually betrayed by Flint’s old quartermaster Long John Silver. The novel would eventually “[infiltrate] the canon of books that every child is supposed to have read” (Furnas 1951: 199) and maintain its appeal through time for readers of all ages (Ward 1975: 305). It has been adapted on screen more than fifty times, including a Muppets movie (1996) and a Disney-produced science fiction retelling (Treasure Planet, 2002). Beyond direct adaptation, the mythology of Treasure Island extends to a robust number of prequels, sequels, and peripheral stories set in the same universe. 

One such reimagining is the television series Black Sails, which aired on the U.S. cable network Starz from January 2014 to April 2017 for a 4-season, 38-episode run. Created by the duo Jonathan E. Steinberg and Robert Levine, Black Sails is set in the West Indies approximately two decades before the events of Treasure Island, while also incorporating several ‘real-life’ pirates and dramatized versions of actual historical events. In the first season, Flint’s crew are on the hunt for the Spanish galleon Urca de Lima which transports a treasure we are meant to understand will eventually find its way into a wooden chest buried on Skeleton Island waiting for Jim Hawkins. Black Sails aims to deconstruct the popular figure of the pirate and recontextualize it not ‘just’ historically but also politically. By the end of season 2, Flint has morphed from a quasi-mythical archetype to a fully fleshed character with an intriguing backstory: the dreaded pirate captain used to be an officer in the British Royal Navy. Disgraced for both his implication in a failed radical project to pardon the pirates of Nassau and his homosexual affair with the architect of said project Lord Thomas Hamilton, he escaped to the Caribbean and turned to piracy. Personally wounded and discarded by his own country, Flint now seeks revenge against England. Seasons 3 and 4 anchor the narrative in the fall of the Republic of Pirates and the end of the Golden Age of piracy: Flint and his crew are at the center of the struggle for the control of New Providence Island against colonial administrator (and eventual Governor of the Bahamas) Woodes Rogers. When Flint makes it to Skeleton Island with his treasure chest, his individualistic thirst for retribution has long since evolved into political consciousness and an explicit collective revolutionary project. Most viewers know the end of the story before they even press play on the very first episode because they have read Treasure Island and because they have at least a vague knowledge of the history of the British Empire: Flint will die, and so will his dream of a free proto-socialist Nassau.   

Despite overall positive critical reviews and eventually acquiring a niche cult following, Black Sails never reached the commercial success of contemporary pay-television series such as Game of Thrones (2012-2019). Most people, when asked about piracy on screen, probably think of Captain Jack Sparrow of the Disney movie franchise Pirates of the Caribbean. Yet the show is particularly interesting as an object of study when it comes to the reception of eighteenth-century Atlantic history, perhaps specifically because of what can account for its lack of mainstream appeal: it is explicitly, unapologetically political. Black Sails is fiction, but it captures a historical reality—a moment of disruption, a revolution in the strictest sense: what historian Amedeo Policante identifies as the transformation of the ocean from a site of absolute freedom into the “first embodiment of the world market” (Policante, 2015: 73).

In “Long John Silver, Karl Marx and the Ship of State”, Loraine Fletcher (2007) argues that very little scholarly attention has been given to Stevenson’s Treasure Island as a political text. She recontextualizes it as both a ‘product of its time’, evidently imbued with the socio-political concerns of Victorian England, but also specifically as intentional, active political commentary—a “rejoinder to Marx’s political philosophy” (Fletcher, 2007: 34). I argue that if Treasure Island is a novel that opposes Marxist internationalism, Black Sails, in turn, reasserts an internationalist commitment not just per se, but specifically as a response to and in dialogue with colonial pirate literature and Treasure Island in particular, which was itself produced in specific historical and ideological circumstances. I build on Fletcher’s analysis to posit that just like Stevenson, Steinberg and Levine utilize the historical setting of the Golden Age to make a political argument on two levels that are dialectically linked: the history that is being represented on the page/on screen, and the moment in which the story is being written/filmed. The relationship between these two levels is not one of analogy or reflection; the present does not simply ‘resemble’ the past, as though history were prone to repeating itself: the present was produced by this past. Atlantic capitalism, the racial order of the plantation complex, and the suppression of collective alternatives have resulted in ongoing structures which continue to organize the world in which Steinberg and Levine created this show. When Black Sails makes arguments about 1715, it is simultaneously making arguments about 2014—not because the two moments mirror each other but because one made the other possible.

The show was produced in documented engagement with the historiography of the eighteenth century Atlantic: Benerson Little, credited as historical consultant on the production, is a prolific popular history writer whose published works on piracy draw on the scholarship of maritime historian Marcus Rediker. The cast and crew’s public statements in the show’s supplemental materials confirm that the engagement runs deeper than surface period detail. Steinberg articulates the show’s historiographical ambition directly: “a lot of people’s understanding of what pirates are is from a story told at their expense. It’s not told from a subjective point of view of what it must have been to be those people” (Bonus Features, S1). This is the project of ‘history from below’ stated as a creative premise. Black Sails, this thesis argues, identifies the Golden Age of piracy and specifically the fall of the Republic of Pirates as a key moment in the establishment of capitalism, and the show is a class-conscious, radical historical commentary of that moment and process. 

Using the show as my central case study, I aim to outline a potential methodology for historians engaging with modern popular interpretations of early America. I have approached Black Sails as a text through the lens of Marxist literary criticism, particularly as outlined by Fredric Jameson in The Political Unconscious (1981); which is to say, a reading of the work (literary in its most extensive sense here to include screenplay and film) not just as reflective of its contemporary culture (superstructure) and mode of production (base) but as containing within itself the seeds that undermine it—reflective instead of the inherent and irreconcilable contradictions of its time. Beyond a new historicist approach that reads the text as ‘contaminated’ by the context it was produced in, and with a real analytical commitment to dialectics, I am interested in a reading that unseals not just the text but the space in which the text is produced: a simplistic pseudo-Gramscian approach sees the cultural production only as a tool of maintenance of the base and thus a reflection of the dominant ideology, but historical materialism posits history as driven and defined by struggle. Artists try to emancipate themselves through their work—with varying degrees of success and through varying degrees of consciousness; artwork is not just determined by its context but also breaking away from it. My analysis is also heavily indebted to Cedric Robinson’s work in Forgeries of Memory and Meaning (2007) and his treatment of film as a central site of struggle in the establishment and maintenance of the American racial regime.

In order to answer the question of how to approach a work like Black Sails as a historian, I borrow from Robert Rosenstone and Hayden White to tackle the question of historiophoty, “the representation of history and our thought about it in visual images and filmic discourse” (White, 1988: 1193). If the written production of the historian is “a verbal structure in the form of a narrative prose discourse that purports to be a model, or icon, of past structures and processes in the interest of explaining what they were by representing them” (White, 1973: 3), it follows that filmic discourse that represents history can also fulfil the same purpose. My work is in line with Rosenstone’s assertion that “all history, including written history, is a construction, not a reflection. [...] That language itself is only a convention for doing history—one that privileges certain elements: fact, analysis, linearity” and thus that “history need not be done on the page. It can be a mode of thinking that utilizes elements other than the written word: sound, vision, feeling, montage” (Rosenstone 1995: 11). As White elaborates, “[e]very written history is a product of processes of condensation, displacement, symbolization, and qualification exactly like those used in the production of a filmed representation” (1988: 1194). The discursive mode of visual representation of historical events, agents, and processes being quite different from that used for their representation in verbal discourse, conceptualizing historiophoty in its own right opens the discussion to an engagement with imagistic material beyond simple ‘illustration’ of the predications made in written discourse .

The historiographical foundation this thesis deploys is drawn from the New Social History of the Atlantic world, and specifically from the strand of that tradition most concerned with recovering the voices and experiences of those at the bottom of the Atlantic order. Marcus Rediker’s Between the Devil and the Deep Blue Sea (1987) and Villains of All Nations (2004) are the central texts: together they reconstruct Atlantic piracy as a form of proletarian resistance, the pirates of Nassau as men and women who were shaped by the violent labor conditions of the Atlantic economy and who built a demonstrable alternative to it. Peter Linebaugh and Rediker’s The Many-Headed Hydra (2000) extends this argument to the broader Atlantic working class—the sailors, slaves, indentured servants, and dispossessed commoners whose cross-racial solidarity constituted the revolutionary threat the colonial order spent the eighteenth century dismantling. Amedeo Policante’s The Pirate Myth (2015) approaches the same terrain from a different angle, tracing the legal and ideological genealogy of the pirate designation—the construction of hostis humani generis as a category placed certain bodies outside the protections of law and made the violence deployed against them appear as administration rather than politics. This thesis reads Black Sails against this body of work, treating them not as background context but as the scholarly standard against which the show’s historiographical arguments are evaluated.

The existing scholarly literature on Black Sails is modest in volume and, with one partial exception, operates outside the Atlantic historiography this thesis mobilizes. The dominant strand is queer theory and representation studies: Walker (2018), Răzman (2020), Schneider (2019), and Carcas (2019) all read the show primarily through the lens of LGBTQ+ identity and visibility, yielding genuine insights into the show’s formal treatment of queer relationships while leaving the political and historical argument largely unexamined. Friedrich’s doctoral thesis (2021) situates Black Sails within a tradition of morally complex television protagonists, reading Flint through the lens of “evil hero” typology—an approach that illuminates the show’s relationship to prestige television conventions without engaging with its historical content. Myrvang’s (2018) comparative study of Silver in Treasure Island and Black Sails is similarly focused on character and adaptation rather than historiography. Dirksen (2019) and Paige (2023) are the most politically alert readings outside this thesis: Dirksen reads Black Sails and Assassin’s Creed IV together through the lens of marginal identities and utopia, while Paige offers a sophisticated feminist legal reading of the show. Both stop short of the historiographical evaluation this thesis undertakes. The partial exception is Swaminathan’s chapter in The Cinematic Eighteenth Century (2018), which situates Black Sails within a broader argument about screen representations of the eighteenth century as a form of public pedagogy—the closest existing work to the historiophoty framework this thesis deploys, though without sustained engagement with the Atlantic scholarship or the show’s specific political arguments. Min-Chi Chen’s (2024) recent work on monstrosity in Black Sails is thematically adjacent to the analytical argument this thesis engages in in its fourth chapter, but it reads it through monster studies rather than through the political history of the figure of the pirate. Jones’s “Black Sails as Philosophy” (2020) is the only piece that engages directly with the show’s political content, but does so through social contract theory rather than through Marxist historiography, producing conclusions that are liberal rather than radical and that leave the class and race dimensions of the show’s argument largely unaddressed. 

The central question this thesis poses is whether a work of commercial prestige television can make legitimate historiographical arguments about the Atlantic past, and what reading it as historiophoty, as an intervention in the ongoing reception of that history, reveals both about the show’s political content and about the practice of history itself.

The thesis proceeds as follows. Chapter 1 establishes the methodological and theoretical foundations. It argues for the study of popular reception as a problem internal to early American history rather than a supplement to it and proposes critical reception theory (adapted from its development in Classical Studies) as the framework for this analysis, and establishes historiophoty as the evaluative tool through which Black Sails is read as a work of historical practice rather than mere entertainment. Chapter 2 establishes the historical and historiographical context: the Atlantic world of the early eighteenth century, the Treaty of Utrecht as a turning point in the consolidation of Atlantic capitalism, and the Republic of Pirates as a documented historical experiment in collective self-governance. Chapter 3 turns to the politics of pirate narratives, tracing the ideological function of the pirate figure from Johnson’s 1724 General History of the Pyrates through Treasure Island, establishing Black Sails as a conscious intervention in this tradition. 

Chapter 4 opens the analytical section with the show's most foundational argument: that the monster narrative deployed against pirates is an ideological operation, and that Black Sails systematically ‘unnaturalizes’ imperial violence by making its structural logic legible as violence, recontextualizing the pirates as operating within a framework of self-defense. Chapter 5 examines the development of collective consciousness across character arcs, reading what literary analysis would call ‘character development’ as the dramatization of a historical and political process, an illustration of consciousness growing out of material conditions and struggle. Chapter 6 addresses race as the primary contradiction within the revolutionary project the show builds, and the show’s sustained commitment to Gramscian pessimism of the intellect and optimism of the will. 

Every point Black Sails makes about the history it represents can be evaluated through and linked back to the work of specific scholars, most often historians. By explicitating these relations, I am presenting the show as a case study of Rosenstone’s argument that the practice of history the way professional historians engage in it, is possible on the screen. I argue that the show does not simply establish its own ideological foundations, it has something to say about the historical processes of the system it criticizes. It identifies the moment in time where things could have gone the other way. This is a deeply historiographical practice. 

The revolutionary optimism this thesis identifies in the show finds its most precise theoretical articulation in Ernst Bloch’s concept of “docta spes”—educated hope, the refusal to mistake historical defeat for structural impossibility (1986)—and in Walter Benjamin’s argument for recovering past moments of resistance from the narrative of inevitable progress (1968). Neither framework is applied systematically in what follows; but they are named here because they describe, more precisely than any other theoretical vocabulary available, what Black Sails is ultimately doing as a work of art.

The final product of this thesis takes the shape of a website within which the scenes I analyze are embedded. The reason for this is twofold: certainly, ease of access to my corpus makes this format most ideal, but most importantly, it reflects the very process of receiving and processing Black Sails that I explore in my thesis. This is a practical application of my core argument: that film media, constrained as it may be by its own limitations, does a type of history that the written word cannot do—and so the Black Sails video extracts I have incorporated within the text serve a purpose that my own written word alone cannot perform.

Front matter

Abstract

This thesis argues that Black Sails (Starz, 2014–2017) performs historiophoty—visual history-making in the sense theorized by Hayden White—by translating Marcus Rediker’s Marxist account of Golden Age Atlantic piracy onto the screen. Reading the series dialectically as a cultural text that works through rather than merely reflects the historical contradictions of its moment of production, the thesis demonstrates that Black Sails makes a sustained historiographical argument about the consolidation of capitalism and the violent suppression of Atlantic proletarian self-organization in the eighteenth century. Conceived as a prequel to Treasure Island, the series is read as a deliberate rejoinder to the Victorian domestication of pirate politics that Stevenson’s novel helped consolidate. The thesis proposes critical reception theory, adapted from its development within Classical Studies, as a methodology for treating popular screen media as a legitimate site of historiographical practice, and intervenes in debates about popular historiography and historical consciousness.

Keywords: Black Sails; historiophoty; critical reception theory; Golden Age of piracy; Atlantic history; maritime capitalism; historical television; Marxist literary criticism
